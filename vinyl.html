What is a Vinyl?

Vinyl records date back to the late 1800s, beginning with Thomas Edison's invention of the phonograph. While early phonographs played audio from wax cylinders, the flat disc format—what we now recognize as vinyl—began to take hold in the early 1900s. These early discs were made from shellac, a brittle material that limited both sound quality and playtime. Still, vinyl quickly became a popular form of home entertainment, allowing people to enjoy music in their own homes like never before.

By the 1980s and '90s, vinyl saw a sharp decline in popularity with the rise of cassette tapes and compact discs (CDs), which offered greater convenience and a more modern appeal. As a result, many music lovers moved on from vinyl in favor of these newer formats. However, in recent years, vinyl has made a notable comeback. Audiophiles and collectors are once again embracing records for their warm sound, tactile nature, and the distinctive listening experience they offer—especially in a world now dominated by digital streaming.

Vinyl Care & Storage

Looking after your vinyl records is essential for preserving their sound quality and ensuring they last for decades.

Storing Your Records

Always store your records in a clean, dry environment, away from extreme temperatures and humidity. Exposure to heat or moisture can cause vinyl to warp, making records unplayable. To prevent warping, store records vertically—standing upright—rather than stacking them flat. The weight from stacking can apply pressure over time and distort the shape of the records.

Handling Vinyl

Handle your vinyl with care by holding it by the edges or the label in the center. Avoid touching the playing surface, as oils from your fingers can settle into the grooves, attracting dust and degrading sound quality. Fingernails can also scratch the grooves, so be gentle and precise when handling your records.

Cleaning Your Records

Just like any cherished item, vinyl records require regular cleaning to maintain their quality. There are two common approaches: dry cleaning and wet cleaning.

1. Dry Cleaning

Use a carbon fiber brush to gently clean your records before and after each play. Brush lightly along the grooves to remove dust and prevent buildup. Double-sided brushes are especially useful—one side reduces static using carbon fibers, while the other (usually a soft cloth) removes remaining dust. Don’t forget to clean the brush itself regularly to prevent residue from transferring back onto your records.

2. Wet Cleaning

For a deeper clean, use a record cleaning solution—either a pre-mixed spray or a diluted fluid blend. Apply with a microfiber cloth, carefully cleaning the grooves, and then dry the record using a separate clean cloth. Wet cleaning is ideal for removing stubborn dirt and restoring clarity to your vinyl's sound.

Turntables & Equipment

A quality turntable and properly aligned cartridge are essential. Consider adding a preamp, good speakers, and vibration control for the best playback experience.
